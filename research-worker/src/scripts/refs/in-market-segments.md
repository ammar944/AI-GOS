# In-Market Segmentation Framework

> Source: Jeremy Haynes — Direct Response Ad Creation Framework SOP (Parts 2-3)
> Applied to AIGOS script generation: map research ICP data to audience readiness tiers

---

## The Three Tiers

### Tier 1: In-Market (3-4% of addressable audience)
- Already believe in the broad category or vehicle
- Don't need "why" — need "which" or "who"
- Evaluating which approach or which expert to pick
- Lowest cost to convert, shortest sales cycles
- **Script tone:** Assume knowledge. No category education. Lead with differentiator.
- **Hook pattern:** "You already [do X]. Here's why [your method] outperforms."

### Tier 2: Needs Convinced (30% of market)
- Know they want the general outcome but not 100% sure the method is correct
- Need more "why" content — higher cost per conversion
- Higher no-show and churn risk
- **Script tone:** Validate the opportunity, then position your approach as the best path
- **Hook pattern:** "You want [outcome] but aren't sure [method] is right. Let me show you."

### Tier 3: Cold Mass (everyone else)
- Not on the table initially
- Converting them first is ultra-expensive
- Causes chaotic lead quality
- **Script tone:** Education-first. Story-driven. Long copy. Build slowly.
- **Hook pattern:** Lead with the world they live in, the frustration they haven't named yet.

---

## Mapping Schwartz Awareness to In-Market Tiers

| Schwartz Level | Primary Tier | Script Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| mostAware | In-Market | Offer-forward. Very short. Urgency without desperation. Just give them a reason to act now. |
| product | In-Market | Handle objections. Reinforce proof. Make action feel safe and obvious. |
| solution | Needs Convinced | Position against alternatives. Explain why THIS approach is different. |
| problem | Needs Convinced | Name the pain precisely. Validate it's real. Then introduce the solution category. |
| unaware | Cold Mass | They don't know the problem. Lead with their world. Story-driven. Build slowly. |

## Sub-Segmentation Within In-Market

Even within the 3-4% in-market, sub-segments exist based on circumstances:

### Sub-Segment A: Current Practitioners
- Already doing the activity (e.g., already running ads, already have clients)
- Want to optimize, scale, or switch to a better method
- **Hook:** "You're already [doing X] but want [better result] without [pain]"

### Sub-Segment B: Adjacent Movers
- Doing something related, curious if this method has better returns
- **Hook:** "You've tried [adjacent method]. [This method] can be the next jump."

### Sub-Segment C: Constraint-Driven
- Have a specific constraint (time, money, regulation) driving the search
- **Hook:** "Your [constraint] means you need [specific approach]. Here's how."

### Sub-Segment D: Ambitious Newcomers (Still In-Market)
- Haven't done a deal yet but are actively searching for a program/mentor
- Already convinced the category is the right path
- **Hook:** "You know [category] is the path. You just need the right [system/guide]."

## How the Planner Uses This

For each script in the 15-script matrix, the planner assigns:

1. **awarenessLevel** (Schwartz): unaware, problem, solution, product, mostAware
2. **inMarketTier**: in-market, needs-convinced, cold-mass
3. **subSegment** (if in-market): current-practitioner, adjacent-mover, constraint-driven, ambitious-newcomer

The hook generator uses the tier + subSegment to select the right opener pattern.
The body writer adjusts depth and proof density based on the tier.

**Default distribution for a 15-script batch:**
- 6 scripts → In-Market (mostAware + product)
- 6 scripts → Needs Convinced (solution + problem)
- 3 scripts → Cold Mass (unaware)

This follows Haynes' principle: **master in-market messaging first, then expand outward.**
